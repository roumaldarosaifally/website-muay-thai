01Origin

Born from
the battlefield.

Muay Thai grew out of Muay Boran, the unarmed combat used by Siamese soldiers when their weapons broke on the battlefield. Villages held matches in dirt pits with rope-wrapped fists. Kings fought. Monks trained. By the 1920s the sport moved into modern rings, adopted timed rounds and boxing gloves — but kept its soul: the ritual, the music, the elbows.

Today it is the national sport of Thailand, practiced in over 100 countries, and the striking foundation for most of modern MMA.

02Style

Eight limbs.
One rhythm.

Fists หมัด — Mahd

Jabs, crosses, hooks, uppercuts — borrowed from Western boxing and sharpened for combinations that set up the heavier weapons.

Elbows ศอก — Sok

Slashing, spearing, horizontal — the elbow is the razor of Muay Thai. Short-range, bone-on-bone, and a common finisher.

Knees เข่า — Kao

Straight knees, curving knees, flying knees. Devastating from the clinch and often the point-scorer that wins rounds.

Shins แข้ง — Kang

The roundhouse kick landed with the shin, not the foot — conditioned by thousands of pad rounds until bone becomes bat.

Scoring

Judges score by round on a 10-point-must system. They reward damage, ring control, technique and balance — a clean kick that lands flush outscores three blocked punches. Knockdowns, teeps that dump an opponent, and dominant clinch work carry the most weight.
